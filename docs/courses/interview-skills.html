Introduction

How to use this course

~5 min read · Start here if it's your first time

This is a self-paced staff-development course on interview coaching. It is not a script to memorize, and it is not a video to watch end-to-end. The point of the course is to change what happens in your next client session — so the value comes from using one module at a time, trying it with two or three clients, noticing what shifted, and coming back for the next module.

A rhythm that works

  1. Read one module. Most take 10–20 minutes.
  2. Try it with your next 2–3 clients. The advice only lands when you've actually used it.
  3. Notice what changed. What worked? What didn't? What would you say differently next time?
  4. Come back a few days later for the next module.

On that pace the whole course takes 6–10 weeks. That's the point. A counsellor who has internalized 16 modules over two months is more useful to their clients than one who binged the course in a single afternoon and remembers none of it on Monday.

What kind of module is in front of me?

  • Modules 1–6 teach the universal mechanics — what an interview is for, how to coach a client through it, formats, STAR, the question bank, and the legal frame (Ontario Human Rights Code + 2026 pay-transparency rules). Read these in order.
  • Modules 7–10 are the deep dives — interview anxiety, disability disclosure, neurodivergent clients, and accommodations. These are reference, not reading: scan to the section that matches the client in front of you.
  • Modules 11–14 cover virtual/AI interviews, presence and body language (with the pop-psych myths debunked), closing strong, and salary conversations under Ontario's new pay-transparency framework.
  • Module 15 is six practice scenarios — best worked through in pairs or as a team.
  • Module 16 is a 15-question self-assessment.
  • Appendices at the end: referral programs and funding (Appendix A), a glossary (Appendix B), a full references list (Appendix C), and a library of ready-to-send scripts and templates (Appendix D).

Module 1

Foundations: What an Interview Is For

~10 min read · Lays the groundwork for every later module

The job of an interview (for the employer)

From the employer's side, an interview is a prediction tool: will this person actually do the job? After a century of industrial-organizational psychology research, the verdict is clear — the format matters enormously. The most-cited meta-analytic estimates of predictive validity (the correlation between a selection method and on-the-job performance) put unstructured interviews around r = .33–.38 and structured interviews around r = .44–.51 (McDaniel, Whetzel, Schmidt, & Maurer, 1994; Schmidt, Oh, & Shaffer, 2016). Work-sample tests outperform both. Combine general mental ability testing with a structured interview and you reach roughly r = .63 (Schmidt et al., 2016).

The practical implication for clients: most modern employers — especially government, healthcare, education, and any employer with an HR team trained in the last decade — are running structured behavioural interviews and scoring you against a rubric. They are not having a conversation. They are filling in a scorecard. Coaching to STAR (Module 4) pays off because that is literally the format the rubric expects.

The job of an interview (for the client)

From the client's side, an interview has two jobs:

  1. Earn the offer. Demonstrate competence, fit, and judgment.
  2. Decide if you want it. Use the room to learn enough about the role, the team, and the manager that you'd accept a "yes" without regretting it later.

Most clients arrive at the interview thinking only of job #1. Part of the counsellor's work is reminding them they're allowed to evaluate the employer too.

Frame for the client "An interview isn't an exam. It's a structured two-way conversation where they're trying to figure out if you can do the work, and you're trying to figure out if you want it. Both of you are gathering evidence."

What "structured behavioural" means in practice

A structured behavioural interview has three features:

  • Every candidate gets the same questions in the same order. This is why "Tell me about yourself" lands the same way for the third candidate as the first.
  • Questions are tied to specific job competencies. "Tell me about a time you handled a difficult customer" is a customer-handling competency probe, not small talk.
  • Answers are scored against a rubric by the interviewer (or by multiple interviewers independently). Anchors like "1 = no example given" through "5 = quantified, specific, well-structured" are common.

Why this matters for our clients

The shift to structured interviewing is broadly good news for clients with barriers. Three reasons:

  1. It rewards preparation. Clients who can't out-charm a polished competitor can still out-prepare one.
  2. It reduces (though does not eliminate) interviewer bias. Anchored rubrics make it harder for "fit" gut feelings to drive the score.
  3. It is teachable. The mechanics of a STAR answer can be drilled. Charisma cannot.

What an interview is NOT

  • Not a confession. A client owes truth, but not full disclosure. There are things employers cannot legally ask (Module 6) and things candidates can choose not to volunteer (Module 8).
  • Not a personality test. Modern hiring has largely moved away from MBTI-style intuitions about "what kind of person are you"; well-trained interviewers ask about past behaviour instead. (For clients who reflexively want to talk about who they "are" — redirect them to what they have done.)
  • Not a one-way evaluation. The last 5–10 minutes — "Do you have questions for us?" — is part of the scorecard. Module 13 covers it in depth.

The counsellor's role in interview prep

Counsellors are not interviewers, and they are not the candidate. The counsellor's job is to:

  • Excavate — surface the client's real, specific accomplishments that will become STAR examples;
  • Rehearse — run mock interviews repeatedly, not once;
  • Translate — help the client describe their work in language an interviewer will recognize;
  • Stabilize — manage the client's anxiety so they can access what they actually know in the room;
  • Coach decisions — disclosure, accommodation requests, salary expectations — without making them for the client.
What we don't do Counsellors do not write the client's answers for them, attend the interview unless explicitly invited as a job coach by the employer, negotiate salary on the client's behalf, or disclose a disability without the client's documented consent. The line between coaching and acting for the client is the line that protects client autonomy.
Module 2

Client-First Mindset

~12 min read · Scope, autonomy, trauma-informed practice

Whose interview is it?

It's the client's. They sit in the room, they answer the questions, they accept or decline the offer. Everything we do as counsellors is in service of their agency, not our preference for how the interview should go.

This sounds obvious. It isn't. The most common counsellor failure mode is over-driving — telling the client what to say, what to disclose, what to accept, what to wear, whether to push back on an illegal question. All of those are choices the client has the right to make differently from how the counsellor would. The counsellor's job is to make sure the client understands the trade-offs.

Three coaching stances that work

  • "Here's what the research shows…" — present evidence (especially for disclosure decisions in Module 8). Then ask: "What feels right for you, knowing that?"
  • "Let's think through both options." — pros/cons on the table, side by side. Don't pre-load your preferred answer.
  • "You don't have to decide that right now." — a lot of pressure in interview coaching is artificial. Some decisions (disclosure, accommodation requests) deserve a day of thinking. Give the client that day.

Trauma-informed interview coaching

Across employment-services caseloads, a meaningful portion of clients carry trauma histories — workplace injury, harassment, illness, incarceration, racial discrimination, gendered violence, migration, displacement. The interview room — closed door, authority dynamics, scrutiny, questions about why employment ended — can re-activate those histories without warning.

Trauma-informed practice in this context means:

  • Predict the trigger. Before a client steps into an interview, name the parts of the format that may land hard ("They'll probably ask why you left your last job. Let's rehearse that one until you don't have to think about it.")
  • Build in choice. Where there's a real choice — in-person vs video, who you tell what to, how much to say — coach the client through it rather than choosing for them.
  • Pace the work. Resume-building in one session is hard; interview rehearsal in one session is harder. Split it across appointments.
  • Have a grounding plan. If a question lands hard in a real interview — for instance, a question that brushes against a difficult prior employment — the client has a script: "Can I take a moment to think?" + one of the in-the-moment techniques in Module 7.
  • Know your scope. If trauma is active and impairing — flashbacks, dissociation, panic — that's the time to refer to trauma-focused therapy in parallel with employment work, not to push through with mock interviews alone.
Language to keep "Can you walk me through what would help here?" beats "Here's what we're going to do." The first invites the client to set the pace; the second tells them what their experience should be.

The seven-second decision myth — what's actually true

You may have heard "interviewers decide in the first seven seconds." It's catchy. It's not what the research says.

Barrick, Swider, and Stewart (2010) found that initial first impressions — formed in the rapport-building moments at the start of a structured interview — correlate meaningfully with final ratings, but the research does not establish a specific second-count, and structured rubrics partially defang the effect. Don't tell clients they have seven seconds to win the job. Do tell them: the opening 60–90 seconds set a frame, and that frame is worth rehearsing — the greeting, the first answer to "tell me about yourself," and the way they hold the silence between the question and their first words.

The "ghostwriter" trap

It's tempting, especially with anxious or under-prepared clients, to hand them a pre-written set of answers. Don't. Three reasons:

  • Memorized answers sound memorized. Interviewers are trained to spot scripted delivery and discount it.
  • The client can't redeploy a script. Question-bank questions are infinite; STAR examples are limited. We're training the client to recombine 5–7 strong examples across many questions — not to recite answer #14.
  • It builds dependence. The client needs to be able to do this for their next job too, without us.

Cultural humility

"Confident eye contact, firm handshake, direct answers" is a culturally specific norm — North American, white-collar, anglophone. For newcomer clients, Indigenous clients, and many clients raised in cultures that valorize deference to authority, the same advice can read as instructions to perform a foreign culture. Coach the norm honestly (it is what the interviewer will expect) but name the trade-off, and give the client room to decide how much of themselves they're willing to translate.

Two horizons, not one

Good interview coaching works on two horizons at the same time. The first is this interview — help the client land the offer in front of them. The second is the next ten interviews — leave the client more equipped to handle their own job search a year from now, without a counsellor in the room. Every coaching decision in this course is judged against both. A move that lands this offer but leaves the client unable to repeat it without you is not actually a win.

Module 3

Interview Formats: Phone, Video, Panel, Async, AI, Working

~12 min read · Match your prep to the format

Every format has its own rhythm, its own pitfalls, and its own coaching priorities. Most clients don't ask the recruiter what format the interview will be — coach them to. ("Could you tell me what to expect — phone screen, video call, panel, or in-person?") The recruiter will tell them, and the answer changes the prep.

Phone screen

  • Who: typically a recruiter, not the hiring manager.
  • What it's for: confirming availability, work authorization, salary range fit, basic competency. A gate, not a deep evaluation.
  • How long: 15–30 minutes.
  • Prep: resume and job description in front of you, a glass of water, a quiet room, headphones with mic if possible.
  • Coaching priority: energy in the voice. Phone removes facial cues, so vocal warmth carries the room. Smile while speaking — it's audible.
  • Pitfall: treating it casually. The recruiter is taking notes; their write-up decides whether the client gets to the next round.

Live video (Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet)

Now the dominant format for first and often second rounds. Module 11 covers the technical setup (camera, lighting, eye contact through the lens). Coaching priorities at the format level:

  • Test the platform 24 hours ahead. Each one handles audio and screen-share differently.
  • Have the recruiter's phone number. If video drops, the client phones in immediately rather than emailing.
  • Headphones reduce echo and let the client hear the interviewer better — important for clients with hearing differences, processing differences, or anxiety.
  • Print the resume. Glancing down at paper reads better on camera than scrolling another window.

One-way (asynchronous) video

The candidate records answers to pre-set prompts with no live interviewer. Used heavily for high-volume early screening. Major platforms include HireVue, Modern Hire (now part of HireVue), and Spark Hire.

  • Format: question appears on screen, candidate gets 30–60 seconds of think-time, then 1–3 minutes to record. Usually 1–2 retakes per question.
  • What's scored: the speech-to-text transcript of your answer is matched against keywords from the job description. Module 11 has the detail on what HireVue does and does NOT do post-2021.
  • Prep:
    • Re-read the job description right before — the keywords you mirror will be matched against it.
    • STAR but tight: roughly 15s situation + task, 60s action, 15s result.
    • Look into the camera lens, not at your own image.
    • Plain wall background, light from the front (window facing you, not behind you), no notifications.
    • Do the platform's tech check if offered.
  • Pitfall: over-rehearsing into rigidity. Async penalizes flat delivery as much as live does.

Panel interview

One candidate, two or more interviewers. Common in government, healthcare, education, unionized environments, and senior roles. The panel is usually some combination of hiring manager + team member + HR. Increasingly common as anti-bias practice — multiple raters mean no single interviewer's gut feel dominates.

  • Logistics to ask the recruiter: how many interviewers, their names and roles, the agenda.
  • Eye contact: address the person who asked the question primarily; sweep the rest of the panel briefly mid-answer; return to the asker for the closing line.
  • Note their names down on a small index card the moment introductions happen. Use names when asking questions back ("Sara, you mentioned the team is restructuring — how is that landing?").
  • Coaching priority: rehearse with two people, not one, even if it's awkward — a friend and the counsellor. The cognitive load of a panel is real.
  • Accommodation note: for autistic clients, clients with social anxiety, and clients with working-memory differences, a single interviewer is a reasonable accommodation request. The panel format is harder for many neurodivergent candidates and it's legitimate to request a 1:1 alternative.

Group interview / assessment centre

Multiple candidates, one or more interviewers, often combined with group tasks. Common in entry-level retail, food service, call centres, sales floors, and graduate trainee programs. The interviewers are watching for leadership emergence, collaboration, conflict tolerance, and stress behaviour.

  • Contribute early. Silent candidates get the worst scores, not the safest ones.
  • Don't dominate. "Speaks well + invites others in" rates higher than "speaks the most."
  • Acknowledge others' contributions by name ("Building on what Priya said…"). Interviewers notice and score it.
  • Coaching priority: for clients with social anxiety, group interviews are often the worst format. If a less-stressful alternative exists for the same role, take it.

Technical / skills interview

Direct demonstration: a coding test, a case study, a role-play sales call, a knife-skills test for a kitchen role, a hands-on welding test for a trades role. Used across industries, increasingly common because it predicts performance better than any verbal answer can (Schmidt et al., 2016).

  • Think out loud. Most technical interviewers are scoring your process, not just the answer. Narrate.
  • Ask clarifying questions. Jumping straight to an answer without confirming the question is penalized.
  • Don't fake. "I haven't used that specific tool, but here's how I'd approach it based on the related work I've done" reads as honest and capable. Made-up jargon does not survive a follow-up question.

Working interview / job try-out

The candidate works a real shift, often paid. Common in trades, food service, salons, dental offices, and small-business service roles.

  • Compensation in Ontario: a working interview is generally compensable time under the Employment Standards Act, 2000. If a client is being asked to work a full shift unpaid, that's worth a conversation — coach them to ask, "Is this a paid trial?" before accepting.
  • Coaching priority: the working interview is a competence test wrapped in a personality test. Show up early, ask one clarifying question per task, accept feedback well, treat coworkers the way you'd treat them on day 30.
  • Accommodation considerations: a working interview can be a great format for autistic and neurodivergent clients because performance, not interview-room performance, is what's scored. It can be a worse format for clients with chronic fatigue or chronic pain who can't sustain a full shift on cold start.

AI-screened video — what it actually is

See Module 11 for the deep dive. The short version: in January 2021, HireVue publicly stopped using facial-expression analysis after internal review and FTC pressure (Maurer, 2021; Mickle, 2021). Modern AI-screening is mostly speech-to-text transcript analysis against the job's keyword model, plus separate game-based assessments. What the candidate says — and how clearly they structure it — is what gets scored. Mirror the keywords from the posting.

Module 4

STAR & Behavioural Preparation

~15 min read · The single highest-leverage skill in interview coaching

If you only have time to teach a client one thing before their interview, teach them STAR. It is the format that structured behavioural interviews are scored against, and a client who can produce a tight STAR answer under pressure has, in one move, leapfrogged most of their competition.

What STAR is — and where it came from

STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result — was developed in the early 1970s by Development Dimensions International (DDI), the leadership-consulting firm founded by William C. Byham, as part of DDI's Targeted Selection behavioural interviewing methodology (Development Dimensions International, n.d.). It has since become the dominant taxonomy taught by employer-side interview training and used in HR scoring rubrics across North America.

Variants exist — CAR (Context, Action, Result), PAR (Problem, Action, Result), SOAR or SOARA (Situation, Objective, Action, Result, Aftermath). Functionally they are the same skill repackaged. STAR is the most-recognized; teach it as the default. The variant worth knowing by name is STAR-L (covered below), which adds an explicit Learning step.

The structure, with timing

  • S — Situation (5–15 seconds). Where, when, role. Set the scene briefly.
  • T — Task (5–15 seconds). The specific responsibility or problem you owned.
  • A — Action (60–90 seconds — the bulk of the answer). What you did, step by step. "I," not "we."
  • R — Result (15–30 seconds). Outcome, quantified where possible, plus the lesson if appropriate.

Total answer: roughly 90 seconds to 2.5 minutes. Beyond three minutes, you've lost the interviewer; under 60 seconds, you've under-answered.

STAR-L — when the Learning step earns its keep

STAR-L adds an explicit L — Learning step at the end. It is the same answer, with one more beat:

  • S — Situation
  • T — Task
  • A — Action
  • R — Result
  • L — Learning (10–20 seconds). What you took from the experience and how it changed how you work now.

STAR-L is not a different format — it is STAR done well. Plenty of strong STAR answers already include a lesson tucked into the Result step. The reason to teach it as its own acronym is that some questions explicitly demand the Learning step, and STAR alone often skips it. Use STAR-L by default whenever the question contains any of these words:

  • "What did you learn?"
  • "Failure," "mistake," "weakness," "regret"
  • "Feedback you found hard to hear"
  • "Conflict," "disagreed with your manager" (the L is what shows growth, not just survival)
  • "Difficult decision," "would do differently"
  • "Adapt," "change at work"

For failure and mistake questions especially, the L is the actual scoring criterion. An interviewer asking "Tell me about a mistake you made" is not asking did you make a mistake (everyone has). They are asking can this person reflect on it without defensiveness, and did they change. A polished R without an L sounds like the candidate moved on without thinking. The L is the answer to the unspoken question.

STAR-L worked example — the "tell me about a failure" question

Question: "Tell me about a time you failed at work."

S: "In my second year as a shift supervisor at a logistics warehouse, our shift was running behind on a peak-week pick target."

T: "I was responsible for hitting daily throughput, and I made the call to redeploy two of our four scanners from receiving over to picking."

A: "I moved the scanners, told the receiving lead, and we hit our picking number that day. But what I didn't do was check the inbound dock's queue before I moved them — three trucks were already waiting, and pulling those scanners stalled them out for the rest of the shift."

R: "We hit picking but missed receiving. Two trucks went over their dwell-time penalty and the operations manager flagged it the next morning."

L: "Two things stuck with me. First, I solved for the metric I was being measured on and stopped looking at the system. Second, the receiving lead would have told me about the queue if I'd asked — I didn't, because I was rushing. Since then, before any resource shift on a shift I run, I do a 30-second check-in with the lead on the other side. It costs nothing and it would have changed the call that day."

What the L step does Notice what the L accomplishes: it shows accountability without grovelling, it shows specific learning (not "I learned to be a better leader"), and it shows the candidate changed their behaviour as a result. That third element is what interviewers are actually scoring on failure questions — the rest is set-up.

STAR-L coaching pitfalls

  • Generic Learning. "I learned that communication is important" doesn't earn a point. The L has to be specific and behaviour-changing.
  • Self-flagellation. The L is not an apology. Brief accountability, then forward.
  • Tacking it on. If the L doesn't connect to the A, the answer reads as rehearsed. Make sure the lesson logically follows from the specific action.
  • Using it everywhere. STAR-L is overkill for "tell me about a time you delivered great customer service." Keep the L for questions that genuinely probe reflection.
The single most common STAR failure Candidates spend 70% of the answer on Situation and Task, then race through Action in one sentence. The interviewer is scoring Action. Fix: ask clients to time themselves and aim for at least 60% of the spoken time inside the Action step.

A worked example

Question: "Tell me about a time you handled a difficult customer."

Weak STAR

"At my last job at the call centre, there was this customer who was really angry. So I, you know, listened to him and tried to figure out what was wrong and we solved it together. It worked out fine in the end. I learned that listening is important."

What's wrong: no specific Situation, "we" instead of "I," vague Action, no quantified Result, generic Lesson.

Strong STAR

S: "In my last role at a residential telecom call centre, a regular customer phoned escalated about a billing error of $340."

T: "I was the agent on the line and our policy was a same-shift resolution for billing disputes under $500."

A: "I pulled his last three statements, identified that a promotional credit had been reversed in error, applied the credit back, walked him through the corrected balance line by line, and offered a one-time goodwill credit to apologize for his time."

R: "The customer stayed on as a client, the call closed in 14 minutes against our 20-minute target, and my supervisor used the recording as a training example for new agents that quarter."

The five-to-seven examples rule

A client doesn't need a STAR answer for every possible question. They need five to seven strong, quantified, polished examples they can redeploy across the entire question bank. Coach the client to surface:

  1. A conflict example (with a coworker, customer, or manager)
  2. A failure or mistake example, with what they learned
  3. A leadership or initiative example (formal title or not)
  4. A problem-solving example (technical or interpersonal)
  5. A customer/client example (going beyond what was required)
  6. A change or pressure example (adapting to deadline, restructure, or crisis)
  7. One "signature story" — the example that captures what makes the client distinctive

Most behavioural questions can be answered by repurposing one of these seven. A conflict example also serves "tell me about a time you persuaded someone" with a different emphasis on the Action step.

Excavating examples from clients who say they have none

The most common moment in interview coaching is the client who says, "I don't have an example." They almost always do. They are filtering for what they think "counts." Reset the filter:

  • "Walk me through your last shift." Concrete, specific, recent. Something will surface.
  • "Tell me about a time something went wrong at work." Everyone has one.
  • "Who at work asked you for help most often, and what did they ask for?" Reveals expertise the client takes for granted.
  • "Outside of paid work — what about volunteer, family, school, sports?" Caregiver organizing six specialists is a coordination story; a youth captaining a team for two seasons is a leadership story; the client coordinating a family wedding is a project-management story. STAR doesn't require a payroll number.

STAR for situational (hypothetical) questions

STAR is the format for behavioural questions — "Tell me about a time when…" For situational or hypothetical questions — "What would you do if…" — STAR doesn't quite fit, because the candidate doesn't have a past event to describe. Use a parallel structure:

  • Situation — what factors I'd consider
  • Approach — what I'd actually do, step by step
  • Resolution — what success looks like, and how I'd know
  • Anchor to a real example — "and that's similar to a situation I handled at [prior role], where…" — bridges back to a STAR you've prepared.

Coaching against memorization

The line between "well-prepared" and "robotic" is real. Three rules:

  • Memorize the outline, not the sentences. The S, T, A, R bullet points for each example — yes. Word-for-word — no.
  • Vary the opening sentence each time the client rehearses the same example. The interviewer is going to ask a slightly different question; the opening has to flex.
  • Practice the transitions. The hard part is the pivot from S+T into A — the "so what I did was…" Drill it.
Exercise — Build five STARs in one session

With a client, set a 60-minute appointment. Build the seven categories above into a 1-page worksheet (or just write the headers). For each category, ask the excavation questions. Capture S, T, A, R as bullet points only — no sentences. Aim to leave the session with at least five examples drafted, even if rough.

Homework for the client: polish them into spoken form before the next session. Run a mock interview in the next session using only those five examples — see if they redeploy across the question bank.

Module 5

The Question Bank

Reference · 60+ questions across 7 categories

This module is reference — don't read it cover to cover. Use it to build a mock-interview question list for a specific client. The wording an interviewer uses will vary, but the underlying construct each question probes is consistent across employers. Coach to the construct, not the exact phrasing.

1. Opening questions

  1. Tell me about yourself.
  2. Walk me through your resume.
  3. Why are you interested in this role?
  4. Why our company?
  5. Why are you leaving (or did you leave) your last job?
  6. Take me through a typical day at your current/last job.
"Tell me about yourself" — coaching A common trap: the client recites their resume chronologically from age 18. Coach the present–past–future structure: one sentence on what you're doing now, one or two sentences on the most relevant prior experience, one sentence on why this role is the next step. Total: 60–90 seconds. Drill it.

2. Behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time when…")

This is the largest category in modern hiring. Each question maps to one of the seven STAR examples from Module 4.

  1. Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a coworker. How did you handle it?
  2. Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager.
  3. Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?
  4. Tell me about a time you made a mistake. What did you do about it?
  5. Describe a time you had to work under pressure or a tight deadline.
  6. Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a customer or client.
  7. Describe a time you had to adapt to significant change at work.
  8. Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news or a difficult message.
  9. Tell me about a time you took initiative without being asked.
  10. Tell me about a time you persuaded someone to your point of view.
  11. Describe a time you had to learn something new quickly. How did you do it?
  12. Tell me about a time you trained or mentored someone.
  13. Tell me about a time you noticed a problem before others did.
  14. Describe a time you had to balance competing priorities.
  15. Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback. How did you respond?

3. Situational / hypothetical ("What would you do if…")

  1. What would you do if you disagreed with your manager's decision?
  2. How would you handle two competing deadlines from two different supervisors?
  3. What would you do if you noticed a coworker doing something unsafe or unethical?
  4. If a customer became verbally aggressive, how would you respond?
  5. How would you prioritize your first 30/60/90 days in this role?
  6. If a teammate wasn't pulling their weight, what would you do?
  7. How would you handle being asked to do something outside your job description?
  8. What would you do in your first week to get up to speed?

4. Competency / technical / job-specific

  1. Walk me through how you would approach [task specific to the job].
  2. What software, tools, or equipment are you proficient with?
  3. Describe your process for [core job function].
  4. How do you stay current in this field?
  5. What's the most complex [project/case/repair/situation] you've handled?
  6. How do you measure your own performance?

5. Weakness, failure, and self-awareness

  1. What is your greatest weakness?
  2. What is something you're working to improve?
  3. What kind of feedback have you received that was hard to hear?
  4. When was the last time you were criticized at work? How did you respond?
  5. What's a professional habit you're trying to change?
  6. What's something most people get wrong about you?
The "weakness" question — what NOT to coach Do not coach the client to say "I'm a perfectionist" or "I work too hard." Interviewers hear that answer six times a week and discount it. Coach a real, modest, work-relevant weakness with a concrete corrective action: "Early in my career I waited too long to ask for help when I was stuck — I worked through it by setting a 20-minute rule, and now I ask sooner." Honest, small, with evidence of growth.

6. Closing questions (from the interviewer)

  1. Where do you see yourself in five years?
  2. What are your salary expectations? (See Module 14.)
  3. When could you start?
  4. Is there anything we haven't covered that you'd like us to know?
  5. Do you have any questions for us? (See Module 13.)

7. Stress and curveball questions

Rarer than older guides suggest, but worth preparing two or three of them so the client isn't thrown.

  1. Sell me this pen / water bottle / [object in the room].
  2. Tell me something not on your resume.
  3. If you were an animal, what would you be and why?
  4. What's a controversial opinion you hold about [field]?
  5. Walk me through a number — how would you estimate the number of [coffee shops in Hamilton / pizzas eaten in Canada per year]?

Coaching: the right move is not a clever answer. It's calm, structured thinking out loud. Interviewers are watching for poise under uncertainty, not creativity.

Building a custom question list

For a specific client, pull 8–12 questions:

  • 2 from Opening
  • 4 from Behavioural — pick the ones whose underlying construct is most relevant to the role
  • 1–2 from Situational
  • 1 from Weakness
  • 1 from Competency/Technical, tailored to the job posting
  • 1 stress/curveball if the role tends toward them (sales, leadership, journalism, consulting)

Knowing Your Rights: Illegal Questions & 2026 Pay Transparency

~12 min read · Ontario Human Rights Code + Working for Workers Four Act, 2024

This module covers what Ontario employers may NOT ask, what they MUST disclose under the 2026 pay-transparency rules, and how a candidate can respond when an interviewer crosses the line. Counsellors should know this material cold — a client who walks into an interview understanding their rights walks in less afraid.

Not legal advice This module is general training for counsellors. It is not legal advice and is not a substitute for the advice of a qualified Ontario lawyer. Statutes, regulations, and tribunal interpretations change. Before a client takes legal action — filing an HRTO application, signing a settlement, responding to a rescinded offer — refer them to the Human Rights Legal Support Centre (free legal help in Ontario; 1-866-625-5179) or to a licensed employment lawyer.

The 14 protected grounds in Ontario employment

Under Part I of the Ontario Human Rights Code, every person has the right to equal treatment in employment without discrimination on the basis of: age, ancestry, citizenship, colour, creed (religion), disability, ethnic origin, family status, marital status, place of origin, race, record of offences, sex (including pregnancy and gender identity / gender expression), and sexual orientation (Ontario Human Rights Commission [OHRC], n.d.-a).

"Employment" is interpreted broadly — full-time, part-time, contract, internships, probationary periods, and even volunteer roles can qualify.

What employers may NOT ask

The OHRC is unambiguous: application forms and interviews must not ask, directly OR indirectly, about any protected ground (OHRC, n.d.-b). Critically — and this surprises many clients — even unintentional questions can establish discrimination. The OHRC's position: the mere fact that an improper question was asked can be sufficient to ground a complaint, even if the candidate is ultimately hired.

The "indirect" trap — questions that probe protected grounds without naming them

This is where clients are most likely to be ambushed. The interviewer means it as small talk; the question is still discriminatory. Common examples:

The disability exception — section 23(3)

The only Code-permitted disability-related inquiry at an interview is whether the candidate is capable of performing the essential duties of the job, with or without accommodation. The lawful form is approximately:

Anything broader is prima facie discriminatory. Module 8 covers disclosure in depth.

Record of offences — Ontario's specific rule

Ontario's Code protects record of offences narrowly: a provincial offence, or a federal offence for which a record suspension (formerly "pardon") has been granted (OHRC, n.d.-c). Convictions without a record suspension are not protected. Employers can lawfully ask about them, and many positions (vulnerable-sector work especially) lawfully require a record check. See the resume course Module 9 for the three Canadian record-check types (CRC, CRJMC, VSC).

What changed January 1, 2026: Working for Workers Four Act, 2024

Ontario's Working for Workers Four Act, 2024 (Bill 149) and its associated regulation introduced major changes to publicly advertised job postings, taking effect January 1, 2026. For employers with 25 or more employees, every publicly advertised posting must:

  1. Disclose expected compensation or a compensation range. If a range is used, it cannot span more than $50,000. The requirement does not apply to roles where expected compensation or the top of the range exceeds $200,000 per year.
  2. Disclose whether AI is used to screen, assess, or select applicants.
  3. State whether the posting is for an existing vacancy or a new role. "Ghost jobs" — postings for roles that don't actually exist — are now prohibited in postings under this Act.
  4. Prohibit any requirement for Canadian work experience in the posting or associated application form.
  5. Notify interviewed applicants within 45 days of the last interview whether a hiring decision has been made.
  6. Retain copies of the posting and the application form for 3 years after the posting is taken down.

Sources: Government of Ontario (2024); Littler (2025); Stikeman Elliott (2025); Fasken (2025).

Common myth — Ontario does NOT have a salary-history ban Unlike British Columbia and Prince Edward Island, Ontario's 2026 framework does not include a ban on asking candidates about prior salary. An Ontario employer can still ask, "What were you earning at your last job?" Many clients have heard otherwise from U.S. media. See Module 14 for how to coach the answer.

How a client can respond to an illegal question (three options)

The legal recourse — a Human Rights Tribunal application — is real, but most clients in an active job search choose one of the de-escalating responses below. Both are valid. The counsellor's job is to present the options and let the client choose.

  1. Redirect — return the question to the job. "I'd be happy to talk about how my experience matches the role. Could you tell me more about what you're looking for in this position?"
  2. Decline politely but firmly — name the off-topic nature without escalating. "That's not really relevant to what I'd bring to the role, but I'd love to address [related competency]."
  3. Name it — for clients prepared to push back. "That question touches on [family status / place of origin], which isn't something I'd want to base a hiring conversation on. Can we move on to the role?"

Filing a complaint — Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario

  • One-year limitation period from the last incident of discrimination. Extensions are rare and require evidence (HRLSC, n.d.).
  • Filing is free.
  • Free legal help is available through the Human Rights Legal Support Centre (HRLSC), an Ontario-funded agency offering advice to anyone in the province regardless of income. Phone (416) 597-4900 / 1-866-625-5179.

Documentation — coach the client to take notes

If a client suspects an illegal question during an interview, contemporaneous notes are decisive at a Tribunal. Coach the client to record, immediately after the interview:

  • Date, time, location (or platform)
  • Names and roles of all interviewers
  • The question that was asked, in the closest-to-exact wording the client can recall
  • The client's response
  • Anyone else present (witnesses)

Preserve the posting, the interview invitation email, and any follow-up. If the offer is rescinded after an illegal question or a disability disclosure, the documentation pattern matters.

A judgment call counsellors face Not every illegal question is malice. Many small employers ask them out of ignorance, not bias. A client may reasonably choose to redirect and still accept the job. Other clients — particularly those in protected groups who have been repeatedly screened out — may reach a point where they no longer want to spend their interview managing the interviewer's biases. Both choices are valid. Don't impose yours.

Interview Anxiety

~20 min read · Reference for clients with significant anxiety

Interview anxiety is one of the largest single barriers between many clients and employment. It is treatable, it is common, and almost everything counsellors can teach about it is supported by good evidence. Read the introduction once, then return to specific sections as clients raise specific issues.

Scope — counsellors are not therapists This module equips counsellors to coach the everyday case and recognize when a client's anxiety has exceeded what coaching can address. We do not diagnose, we do not provide therapy, and we do not advise on medication. When the pattern in front of you looks clinical, refer (see "When to refer" below) — in parallel with employment work, not instead of it.

What interview anxiety actually is

Interview anxiety is best understood as a situation-specific blend of performance anxiety and social-evaluative anxiety. McCarthy and Goffin's foundational measurement work — the Measure of Anxiety in Selection Interviews (MASI) — identifies five distinct dimensions: communication anxiety, appearance anxiety, social anxiety, performance anxiety, and behavioural anxiety (McCarthy & Goffin, 2004). The five-dimension structure matters clinically: a client whose anxiety is dominated by appearance concerns benefits from different interventions than one whose anxiety is dominated by communication.

Does it actually hurt outcomes?

Yes. Powell, Stanley, and Brown's meta-analysis of 41 independent samples found a population correlation of r = –.19 between self-reported interview anxiety and interview performance — anxious candidates score systematically lower despite often being equally qualified (Powell et al., 2018).

Survey statistics floating around the popular literature ("92% of adults have interview anxiety") come from non-peer-reviewed industry polling. Useful for framing — "you are not alone" — but don't cite them as research.

Frame for the client "Interview anxiety is the norm, not a defect. The research question isn't whether you'll feel it. It's how to keep it from costing you the job."

The psychophysiology — why your client "blanks"

Interview anxiety triggers sympathetic nervous-system activation: elevated heart rate, shallow breathing, peripheral vasoconstriction, dry mouth, tremor, cortisol release. The cognitive consequence is what Eysenck, Derakshan, Santos, and Calvo articulated in Attentional Control Theory (2007): anxiety preferentially consumes the working-memory and inhibitory-control resources needed for goal-directed behaviour. Anxious people can often still perform — but they pay a much higher processing cost. This is why a well-prepared client can suddenly "blank" on a question they knew the answer to.

When it crosses into clinical territory — Social Anxiety Disorder

Per the DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2022), Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) requires: marked, persistent (≥6 months) fear of one or more social situations involving possible scrutiny; fear of negative evaluation; the situation almost always provokes fear; active avoidance or endurance with intense distress; fear out of proportion to actual threat; clinically significant distress or functional impairment.

A counsellor's job-relevant red flag: a client who has turned down or no-showed multiple interviews because of anticipatory dread, or who reports the anxiety has lasted ≥6 months and bleeds into other social domains, should be supported toward a clinical assessment in parallel with employment coaching.

Evidence-based techniques (teach with confidence)

1. Cognitive restructuring (CBT)

The strongest evidence base of any technique on this list. CBT for social and performance anxiety produces large effect sizes that hold at 6–12 month follow-up (van Dis et al., 2020). The core move: identify catastrophic automatic thoughts ("I'll freeze and they'll laugh"), test them against evidence, replace them with balanced alternatives ("I might stumble, but I've recovered from that before, and most interviewers don't notice the stumbles I do").

For clients without therapist access, Anxiety Canada's My Anxiety Plan (MAP) is a free, evidence-based CBT self-help program structured around thought-challenging worksheets — appropriate for mild-to-moderate symptoms (Anxiety Canada, n.d.).

2. Graded exposure (mock interviews done right)

Exposure is the workhorse intervention. One mock isn't exposure — it's a sample. Effective exposure is graded, repeated, and includes the feared elements (a stranger interviewer, time pressure, the question the client dreads, video on). Expect anxiety to rise during early reps and habituate across sessions. Coach the client to stay in the discomfort — fleeing the feared element re-confirms the fear.

3. Slow-paced breathing

The honest summary: slow-paced breathing reliably reduces acute physiological arousal, but no specific protocol (box breathing, 4-7-8) is differentially superior to any other slow nasal breathing under ~6 breaths per minute (Fincham et al., 2023). Slow exhalation activates the vagus nerve, shifts autonomic balance toward parasympathetic.

Teach a protocol — the simplest is 4 in, hold 1, 6 out — because slow breathing works. Skip the marketing ("Navy SEAL breathing") — the SEAL provenance is anecdotal, not from controlled trials.

4. Reappraising anxiety as excitement

Well-evidenced and easy to teach. Brooks (2014) showed across multiple studies — karaoke singing, public speaking, math performance — that participants who said "I am excited" outperformed those who said "I am calm" or said nothing. The shift reframes arousal from a threat signal to an opportunity signal without trying to suppress it (which usually backfires).

Jamieson and colleagues' arousal-reappraisal work extends this: telling people that a racing heart is the body preparing them — not failing them — improves cardiovascular stress profiles and reduces evaluation anxiety (Jamieson et al., 2012).

Coach the client to say (out loud, before the interview)"My heart is racing. That's my body getting me ready. I am excited."

5. Implementation intentions ("if X then Y")

Strong evidence base. Bieleke, Keller, and Gollwitzer (2024) summarized 642 tests showing medium-to-large effects from if-then plans. For interviews, build 3–4 if-then plans into the client's prep:

  • "If I don't know the answer, then I will say 'That's a great question — can I take a moment to think?'"
  • "If my mind goes blank, then I will sip my water and breathe out slowly."
  • "If I notice myself rambling, then I will pause, say 'Let me bring that back to your question,' and stop."
  • "If they ask a question I haven't prepared, then I will use my problem-solving example and adapt it."

6. Self-compassion / ACT-based acceptance

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) and self-compassion practices treat anxiety not as an enemy to defeat but as a sensation to allow while still acting on values. Meta-analyses show ACT is roughly equivalent to CBT for anxiety and depression (Gloster et al., 2020). The practical phrase: "You don't have to feel ready. You just have to show up and act on what matters to you."

7. Grounding (5-4-3-2-1 and cold water)

The 5-4-3-2-1 sensory exercise (name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste) has modest emerging evidence and is widely adopted because it's low-risk, free, and uses no equipment. Cold water on the face triggers the mammalian dive reflex — rapid parasympathetic activation. Both are rescue tools, not stand-alone treatments. Useful in the parking lot before walking in.

DO NOT teach as effective

"Power posing" as a hormonal intervention. The original Carney, Cuddy, and Yap (2010) finding — that two minutes of "power posing" raises testosterone and lowers cortisol — has failed multiple high-powered replications (Ranehill et al., 2015). In September 2016, first author Dana Carney publicly stated: "I do not believe that 'power pose' effects are real" (Carney, 2016). What has held up modestly is a small self-perception effect on felt confidence. If a client finds standing tall briefly helpful, fine — frame it as posture cueing confidence, not as a biological hack. Do not repeat the testosterone/cortisol claim.

8. Beta-blockers (propranolol) — brief mention, refer to prescriber

Propranolol is prescribed off-label for situational performance anxiety. It blunts somatic symptoms — tremor, racing heart, dry mouth — without sedation; it does not reliably reduce cognitive symptoms (Steenen et al., 2016). Some clients with severe physical symptoms may benefit. This is a conversation with the family doctor or nurse practitioner, never a counsellor recommendation. Contraindications (asthma, certain heart conditions) require prescriber assessment.

The night before and the day of

  • Sleep. One night of sleep deprivation amplifies amygdala reactivity to negative stimuli (Yoo, Gujar, Hu, Jolesz, & Walker, 2007). Protect 7–8 hours. No alcohol — it fragments REM.
  • Caffeine. Same dose as a normal day, ideally a bit less. Never first-time experiment with a new energy drink. High doses (≥400 mg/day) measurably increase anxiety; in panic-disorder patients even moderate doses can provoke attacks (Liu et al., 2024).
  • Hydration. Mild dehydration worsens working memory.
  • Visualization. Sport-psych meta-analyses show consistent small-to-moderate effects when the rehearsal is specific — imagining the exact room, opening question, answer, recovery from a stumble.
  • Arrival. 10–15 minutes early. Earlier becomes anticipatory dwell time, which inflates anxiety per ACT (Eysenck et al., 2007).

In the room — moment-to-moment

  • The pause. A 3–5 second silence before answering is universally read as thoughtful, not awkward. Coach the client out of the impulse to fill silence with "um, so, basically…"
  • The water sip. A sip is a socially invisible 5-second pause. Have the client bring a bottle.
  • "Can I take a moment to think?" — endorsed in practitioner literature as a strong signal of self-regulation.
  • Recovery from a flub. Acknowledge briefly, redo, move on. Do not apologize repeatedly — it signals low confidence and draws attention back to the error. Clean line: "Let me restart that answer — what I really want to say is…"

Panic attack mid-interview — what it looks like, what to coach

A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear peaking within minutes: palpitations, shortness of breath, derealization, fear of dying or going crazy (DSM-5-TR). Crucially, panic attacks pass. The peak is typically 5–10 minutes.

  1. Name it silently. "This is a panic attack. It will peak in 5–10 minutes. It will pass."
  2. Slow the exhale. Longer out than in. 4 in, 6–8 out.
  3. 5-4-3-2-1 grounding. Internal; the interviewer won't notice.
  4. Request a break if needed. "Could I have a five-minute break? I'd like to get a glass of water." This is a reasonable request — it doesn't require disclosing a condition.
  5. The goal is not to prevent the panic. It's to not catastrophize it. Many candidates have had a panic attack mid-interview and still received the offer.

Specific subtypes to flag

  • Selective mutism in adults — rare but real. A client who can speak fluently with you but consistently freezes in interviews to the point of being unable to produce speech is beyond standard interview coaching — refer.
  • PTSD — interview rooms (closed doors, authority dynamics, certain question patterns, specific demographic interviewers) can trigger. Coach scouting the space, sitting nearest the door, having a grounding plan. If trauma is active, parallel trauma-focused therapy is the right path.
  • OCD — preparation rituals that become functionally impairing (re-reading the job description 40 times, repeated outfit checks). Refer to CBT with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP).

Mock-interview structure that actually works

  • Multiple sessions — at least three. One won't habituate the anxiety.
  • Realism — a stranger interviewer when possible; formal setting; video on; real timing.
  • Feedback targeting specific behaviours (eye contact, answer structure, recovery), not vague "you did great."
  • Recording and reviewing — but only if the client finds review useful. For some autistic clients, watching themselves on video amplifies masking pressure; skip it.

When to refer — Ontario pathways

Refer when you see any of: avoidance lasting ≥6 months, anxiety bleeding into other social domains, panic attacks tied to interviews, trauma surfacing in coaching, sleep disturbance / intrusive thoughts / hypervigilance, or a client self-reporting a prior anxiety / panic / OCD / PTSD diagnosis.

Disability Disclosure

~25 min read · Whether, when, and how — the most-requested coaching topic

Whether, when, and how to disclose a disability during hiring is one of the most consequential decisions a client faces. It is also one of the most poorly coached, because counsellors are often working from rules that aren't quite right (Canadian law is not American law) or from a default ("don't disclose") that is right for many situations but wrong for others. This module gives the law, the decision framework, the scripts, and the limits of our role.

Not legal advice

The legal sections below summarize Ontario's framework for general professional-development purposes. They are not legal advice. They are not a substitute for a qualified Ontario lawyer, and they do not create a lawyer–client relationship between the course authors, the reader, or any client.

When a client is making a real legal decision — filing a Human Rights Tribunal application, responding to a rescinded offer, signing an accommodation agreement, negotiating a settlement — refer them to the Human Rights Legal Support Centre (free; 1-866-625-5179) or to a licensed employment lawyer. Counsellors coach; lawyers advise.

The legal framework in Ontario (2026)

The Ontario Human Rights Code

For provincially regulated employers — most Ontario workplaces — the operative statute is the Ontario Human Rights Code. Disability is a protected ground. The Code's definition (section 10(1)) is intentionally broad, covering past, present, perceived, and episodic disabilities, including mental-health disabilities and learning disabilities (OHRC, 2016).

Key sections:

  • Section 5(1) — every person has a right to equal treatment in employment, broadly defined (full-time, part-time, contract, volunteer, internship, probation).
  • Section 11 — constructive (indirect) discrimination. A neutral rule that excludes people because of disability is discriminatory unless the employer can establish a bona fide occupational requirement (BFOR).
  • Section 17 — a Code right is not infringed where the person cannot perform essential duties even with accommodation, but no decision-maker can find incapacity unless needs cannot be accommodated without undue hardship.
  • Section 23(2)–(3) — application forms and pre-employment inquiries that classify or indicate qualifications by a Code ground are prohibited; section 23(3) carves out a narrow exception allowing employers, at a personal interview, to ask whether the applicant has disability-related needs to perform essential duties.

The Meiorin test (duty to accommodate)

The leading Supreme Court of Canada authority is British Columbia (Public Service Employee Relations Commission) v. BCGSEU ("Meiorin"), 1999. The unified three-part BFOR test requires the employer to show:

  1. The standard was adopted for a purpose rationally connected to job performance;
  2. It was adopted in honest, good-faith belief it was necessary; and
  3. It is reasonably necessary to accomplish that purpose — including that it's impossible to accommodate the claimant without undue hardship.

Undue hardship — what actually counts

Under section 17(2), the only factors a decision-maker considers are cost, outside sources of funding, and health and safety. The OHRC's Policy on ableism and discrimination based on disability makes clear the threshold is high — "business inconvenience," employee morale, third-party preferences, and customer reactions are not recognized grounds (OHRC, 2016).

AODA / IASR — the Employment Standard

The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005 and its Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (O. Reg. 191/11) require Ontario employers to:

  • Notify the public and employees that accommodation is available in recruitment;
  • Notify applicants individually, when invited to assessments, that accommodation is available on request, and consult with the applicant to provide suitable accommodation;
  • Notify successful applicants, at offer, of the employer's accommodation policies;
  • For employers with 50+ employees, develop written individual documented accommodation plans (IAPs) under section 28 of IASR.

A candidate's request for an interview-stage accommodation is not a favour — it is a procedural right.

Federal framework — only for federally regulated employers

The Canadian Human Rights Act and Accessible Canada Act, 2019 apply only to federally regulated employers — banks, federal public service, telecoms, interprovincial transportation, postal service, Crown corporations. For clients applying to those employers, this framework — not the Ontario Code — governs (Government of Canada, 2023).

The "no surprise" point — emphasize this with clients A candidate may request accommodation for the interview/application process itself without committing to ongoing disclosure on the job. The two are legally and practically distinct. Requesting captioning for a virtual interview, for example, discloses nothing about how the client would perform the essential duties.

What employers may NOT ask about disability

Pre-offer, employers cannot ask: whether the applicant has a disability or specific medical condition; sick-leave history with prior employers; past or current prescriptions or treatments; whether the applicant has filed WSIB claims or human-rights complaints; "out of curiosity" questions like "How did you end up in a wheelchair?"

The only Code-permitted inquiry at the interview is: "Can you perform the essential duties of this job, with or without accommodation?"

Post-offer medical examinations are permitted only if (a) job-related, (b) applied uniformly to all successful candidates for that role, and (c) limited to what's needed to assess fitness to perform essential duties. Information must be confidential and used only for accommodation planning (OHRC, n.d.-d).

Disclosure — the decision framework

Whose decision is it?

The client's. Disclosure may be full, partial, or withheld, and the choice may be made differently at different stages (CCRW, n.d.; JVS Toronto, n.d.). Our job is to present the evidence and the trade-offs, not to decide for the client.

When disclosure becomes essentially required

  • An accommodation is needed during the application or interview (interpreter, large print, extended time, accessible venue);
  • The essential duties cannot be performed without accommodation the employer must arrange;
  • Safety is implicated under a legitimate BFOR (certain commercial driving, regulated safety-sensitive positions).

The disclosure script — three moves

CCRW's Writing a Disclosure Statement course and JVS Toronto's guidance both teach a three-part frame:

  1. Name the disability or condition in plain language. No medical detail needed.
  2. Name the specific accommodation needed (or confirm none is).
  3. Frame around capacity — what the candidate brings; how the accommodation enables performance of essential duties.
Template "I have [condition]. For the work in this role I use [accommodation], which I'll bring with me / which I'd like to arrange ahead of time. With that in place, I can fully perform the duties we've discussed — and [strength relevant to the role] is part of why I've succeeded in [prior context]."

Noticeable disabilities — different calculus

When a disability is noticeable to the interviewer — wheelchair, mobility aid, white cane, guide dog, prosthetic, AAC device, facial difference, noticeable scar, tic, observable tremor — the candidate doesn't control whether the interviewer notices. The choice is only whether and how to address it.

A note on language: this course uses "noticeable" and "not noticeable" rather than the more common "visible" and "invisible." Some disabilities (a deaf candidate who speaks, a tic disorder that flares mid-interview, an AAC user) are noticed through means other than sight — and the framing "noticeable to the person across from you" captures the social dynamic more accurately than "visible." Sources cited in this section sometimes use the older "visible / invisible" terminology; that's preserved in citations and reference titles.

The literature supports brief proactive naming. Research at York University (Lindsay et al., 2018) found that candidates who briefly named a noticeable disability and pivoted to capacity outperformed candidates who either ignored the difference (leaving the interviewer distracted) or who over-explained.

Noticeable-disability opening (sample for wheelchair user) "You'll notice I use a power wheelchair. For the duties we've discussed, my current setup works without any changes from you; for [specific duty], I'd use [specific accommodation]. Happy to answer any logistics questions."

Service animals (Ontario)

Ontario's Blind Persons' Rights Act makes it an offence to deny a guide-dog handler access to facilities and services available to the public — but it narrowly protects vision-disability handlers using a guide dog. For other service animals, protection flows from the Ontario Human Rights Code's duty to accommodate, not the BPRA. There is no provincial certification scheme for service animals beyond guide dogs (ARCH Disability Law Centre, n.d.; CNIB, n.d.).

Not-noticeable disabilities

Mental health conditions, chronic pain, chronic illness, neurodivergence, low-vision or hard-of-hearing that isn't obvious, cognitive effects from TBI or stroke, learning disabilities, HIV. The disclosure calculus is different:

  • Passing privilege has a cost. Qualitative research documents the energy cost of masking and the mental-health toll of concealment, particularly for autistic adults and people with mental-health disabilities (Brouwers, 2022; Hull et al., 2021).
  • Episodic disabilities (MS flares, mental-health relapses, migraine, lupus, long COVID, Crohn's) sit awkwardly in disclosure decisions because the candidate may be currently asymptomatic. MS Canada advises that if a candidate chooses to disclose, disclosure should be limited to symptoms that affect the work (MS Canada, n.d.).
  • Stigma data. Mental-health disabilities carry the highest stigma load. The Mental Health Commission of Canada's Opening Minds program documents persistent workplace stigma (Stuart et al., 2014).

Brouwers's (2022) meta-ethnography found disclosure outcomes are mediated by perceived workplace culture, supervisor relationship, and the candidate's reasons for disclosing — with avoidance-motivated disclosure (fear-driven) producing worse outcomes than approach-motivated disclosure (to obtain accommodation, to live authentically).

Mental health conditions

General guidance from MHCC research is that disclosure is most beneficial when (a) an accommodation is needed, (b) the workplace has visible signals of psychological safety (anti-stigma policies, employee resource groups, mental-health champions), and (c) the candidate has a supportive supervisor — usually unknowable at the interview stage. Default for many clients: do not disclose pre-offer unless an interview accommodation is needed; revisit post-offer if needed for ongoing accommodation.

Deaf and hard-of-hearing candidates

  • Common accommodations: ASL or LSQ interpreter, CART captioning, written follow-up summaries, well-lit room with the speaker facing the candidate, written interview questions in advance.
  • Request through the recruiter or HR contact (not the hiring manager) as soon as the interview is booked.
  • Resources: Canadian Hearing Services.

Vision loss and blindness

  • Confirm navigation logistics ahead of time (entrance, route to interview room).
  • Request materials in accessible formats (electronic with screen-reader compatibility, large print, Braille).
  • Under Ontario's Blind Persons' Rights Act, guide-dog access cannot be refused.
  • Resource: CNIB.

Chronic illness and chronic pain

Schedule flexibility, ergonomic seating during long interviews, water and breaks. Disclosure depends on whether essential duties can be performed without accommodation. The MS Canada principle — disclose only symptoms that affect the work — generalizes well.

Acquired brain injury, stroke, post-COVID cognitive

Cognitive fatigue, word-finding difficulty, slowed processing speed. Schedule interviews mid-morning when possible; request shorter formats (45 min vs 90) or split sessions; prepare key terminology in advance; have a recovery script: "Give me a moment — the word will come." Brief acknowledgment normalizes the pause (University of Washington TBI Model System, 2025).

Stuttering and dysfluency

Strong evidence base for brief, proactive disclosure at the start of the interview to neutralize interviewer distraction. The Canadian Stuttering Association and the Stuttering Foundation both recommend an early acknowledgement.

Stutter disclosure opening "Before we start, I want to mention that I stutter — so you may hear some repetitions or pauses. It doesn't affect my skills. Feel free to ask me to repeat anything."

What to do if you suspect discrimination

  • Red flags: illegal questions about disability or medical conditions; sudden cooling of interviewer demeanour after disclosure; offer rescinded after an accommodation request; "this might not be the right fit" tied to disability content.
  • Document immediately: date, time, names, exact wording, your response, witnesses. Preserve emails and posting.
  • HRTO limitation: applications must be filed within one year of the last incident. Filing is free.
  • Free legal help: Human Rights Legal Support Centre (HRLSC), independent Ontario-funded agency — (416) 597-4900 / 1-866-625-5179. Available to anyone in Ontario regardless of income.

We do not file complaints, advise on legal strategy, or speak to employers about discrimination claims unless explicitly engaged as a job coach with the client's documented consent.

  • JVS Toronto — disability employment programs and disclosure coaching.

Neurodivergent Clients

~18 min read · Autism, ADHD, learning disabilities, dyspraxia, Tourette, acquired cognitive

Traditional interviews systematically penalize neurodivergent candidates. They over-weight social fluency, eye contact, abstract hypotheticals, small talk, and "fit" — the very things that are not core to most jobs. This module gives counsellors the evidence-based corrections.

The structural problem (and the corrective shift)

Sasson and colleagues (2017) demonstrated empirically what experienced job coaches have long observed. In a series of thin-slice studies, neurotypical observers rated autistic adults less favourably across multiple trait dimensions — and expressed reduced willingness to interact with them — within seconds of exposure. Critically, these biases disappeared when only conversational content was presented, with audio-visual cues removed: "style, not substance, drives negative impressions of ASD" (Sasson et al., 2017). A follow-up study found first impressions improved when observers were informed of the candidate's autism diagnosis (Sasson & Morrison, 2019).

The corrective shift in major employer practice is toward: (a) structured behavioural interviews, (b) work-sample tasks as the primary screening tool, and (c) dedicated neurodiverse hiring tracks that replace or supplement traditional interviews.

Canadian employment context: the 2022 Canadian Survey on Disability found 62% employment among working-age adults (25–64) with disabilities, compared with 78% of adults without disabilities (Statistics Canada, 2023). Autism-specific figures vary widely across sources — directional finding (significant underemployment of autistic adults) is well-established; specific percentages should be cited carefully.

Autism Spectrum

Eye contact, body language, and the masking trap

Atypical eye contact is one of the most noticeable markers producing negative interviewer impressions (Sasson et al., 2017). The temptation is to coach the client to "perform" eye contact. The evidence cautions against this as a primary strategy. Camouflaging — actively suppressing autistic behaviour to appear neurotypical — is robustly associated with anxiety, depression, and exhaustion (Hull et al., 2021). Raymaker et al. (2020) describe how chronic masking contributes to autistic burnout, characterized by exhaustion, skill loss, and reduced tolerance to stimulation.

A more defensible coaching frame:

  • Practise functional eye-contact substitutes (looking at the interviewer's forehead, mouth, or shoulder) only enough to avoid signalling disengagement — not as a sustained performance.
  • Where the client is willing, pre-interview disclosure is supported by evidence: Sasson and Morrison (2019) and Whelpley and May (2023) found that disclosing an autism diagnosis improves interviewer ratings of candidate performance.
  • Name the trade-off honestly: masking may help in the moment, but the cumulative cost is real.

Literal interpretation and abstract questions

Autistic candidates often answer abstract or hypothetical questions literally ("Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" → "At my desk"). Norris, Crane, and Maras (2020) showed that autistic adults' recall is reduced under open-ended questioning but improves substantially with semantic prompts and a who/what/where/when framework.

Coaching application: rehearse converting hypotheticals into behavioural equivalents. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" → answer it as "Tell me about a time you set a goal at work and what you did to reach it."

Processing time, questions in advance, special interests

Autistic candidates often need additional time to process and formulate responses. EARN, Autism Ontario, and major neurodiverse hiring programs recommend requesting questions in advance (15 minutes to 24–48 hours). Frame deep interests as evidence of capacity for focus and domain expertise — a recurring theme in employer reports from neurodiverse hiring programs at SAP, Microsoft, JPMorgan, and EY.

Sensory environment

Fluorescent lighting, perfume, HVAC hum, and panel-room echo can degrade autistic candidate performance independent of skill. Reasonable requests include a quiet room, controllable lighting, and a single interviewer rather than a panel.

Employer programs to know

  • Specialisterne Canada — replaces traditional interviews with skills-based exercises and a working-trial week; partners with TD, RBC, and others.
  • Ready, Willing and Able — Canadian national program for candidates with intellectual disability or on the autism spectrum.
  • Autism Career Connections (Autism Ontario) — free self-paced and in-person workshops including mock interviews.
  • International programs: SAP Autism at Work, Microsoft Neurodiversity Hiring Program, JPMorgan Autism at Work, EY Neurodiversity Centers of Excellence.

ADHD

Strengths to lead with (without overclaiming)

Hyperfocus, divergent thinking, crisis responsiveness, comfort with novelty and parallel tasks. Coach the client to ground each claim in a specific example — interviewers discount unsupported strength claims, and ADHD candidates can fall into broad generalisation under stress.

Common interview challenges and strategies

Tangential answers, interrupting, losing the thread of a question, fidgeting, forgetting prepared examples (CADDAC, n.d.). Coaching strategies:

  • STAR-format index cards, one per story, with the single key sentence at the top.
  • Pause before answering — a 3–5 second deliberate pause defeats the impulse to start before the question is fully processed.
  • Pre-interview movement to discharge motor energy (a brisk walk before arriving).
  • Permission to take notes during the interview to externalize working memory.
  • Repeat the question back briefly ("So you're asking about a time I handled a tight deadline...") — slows the response, confirms understanding, retrieves the prepared story.

Medication

Do not advise clients on medication timing or changes — refer to their prescriber. Counsellors can legitimately help a client think through whether their interview is at a time of day when they're typically more focused, but stop short of any clinical advice.

Learning Disabilities (Dyslexia, Dyscalculia, Dysgraphia)

LDs typically affect specific cognitive processes (decoding, numerical calculation, written output, working memory) without affecting general intelligence (LDAO, n.d.; Job Accommodation Network, n.d.).

  • Dyslexia: request the job description and any pre-interview reading in advance; allow extra time for forms; consider screen-reader software. Reading-aloud tasks during interview — request silent reading or oral discussion. Written tests — extra time and/or oral administration are reasonable accommodations.
  • Dyscalculia: numerical reasoning tests, "estimate the answer" questions, and salary-negotiation math under pressure are predictable trouble spots. Accommodations: calculator access, extended time. Rehearse salary conversations with concrete numbers written down in advance.
  • Dysgraphia: handwritten tests — request a keyboard. For forms requiring signatures or handwritten content, alternatives include typed forms or scribed responses.
  • Working memory: multi-part interview questions ("Tell me about a time you... and what did you learn... and how would you apply it here?") overwhelm working memory. Coach clients to politely ask for the question to be repeated or broken into parts.

Dyspraxia / Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD)

DCD is under-recognised in adults and frequently co-occurs with ADHD and autism. Common interview-relevant issues: handshake awkwardness, dropping materials, navigating an unfamiliar building, difficulty organising notes or pens on a table, slower speech production when planning motor movement.

  • Practise the handshake (or pre-disclose that you prefer not to shake hands — widely accepted post-pandemic).
  • Visit the building in advance or request a floor plan / interviewer name in advance.
  • Bring as few physical materials as possible; use a single folder rather than loose pages.
  • Request task-based rather than question-only interviews where possible.

Tourette Syndrome and Tic Disorders

Coprolalia (involuntary swearing) is a minority experience — most estimates around 10% — with broader estimates ranging up to 33% depending on the cohort and definition. Most people with Tourette do not have coprolalia; coaches should be ready to address that misconception with employers.

Pre-disclosure

Tourette Canada and the Tourette Association of America generally recommend a brief pre-interview disclosure to short-circuit interviewer surprise.

Tourette disclosure opening "Before we start, you may notice some involuntary movements or sounds — those are tics. They don't affect my ability to do the job, and they often decrease once I'm focused on the work."

Tic suppression cost

Many people with Tourette can suppress tics for short periods, but suppression builds a premonitory urge that rebounds — often resulting in a tic cluster immediately after the interview, or worsening tics late in a long interview. Coach clients to:

  • Request a shorter or split interview format if possible.
  • Take a brief break mid-interview if needed.
  • Not over-rely on suppression as a strategy.

Acquired conditions (TBI, stroke, MS, post-COVID cognitive)

These conditions share three interview-relevant features: cognitive fatigue, word-finding difficulty, and slowed processing speed.

  • Scheduling: cognitive fatigue is often worst late in the day. Mid-morning interviews are widely recommended.
  • Format: request shorter blocks (45 min vs 90) or split interviews across two days; request breaks every 20–30 minutes. For clients with light sensitivity post-TBI, in-person may be easier than video.
  • Word-finding strategies: prepare key terminology in advance and bring a written list; use the interviewer's own terminology back; have a recovery script.

Coaching practice — what works for neurodivergent clients

  1. Strengths inventory first — five to seven concrete STAR examples the client can redeploy. Build the inventory before the first mock interview.
  2. Question-bank rehearsal — common behavioural questions, each tied to one of the prepared examples. Rehearse out loud to the point of automaticity.
  3. Opening and closing scripts — locked-down greeting and goodbye (the highest-stress moments).
  4. Recovery scripts — "Can you repeat the question?"; "Give me a moment to think"; "The word will come"; "Let me come back to that."
  5. Sensory simulation — practise in the actual lighting / room type the interview will use, if possible.
  6. Recorded review only if useful — for some autistic clients, watching themselves on video amplifies masking pressure; skip it.

The job-coach-as-bridge approach

With the client's documented consent, a counsellor briefing an employer ahead of the interview — explaining the diagnosis, what accommodations help, what the client's strengths are, and what the interviewer might otherwise misinterpret — can dramatically improve outcomes. This is the operating model of Specialisterne Canada and Ready, Willing and Able. Document the consent in writing before contacting the employer.

Things NOT to do

  1. Do not push masking as a strategy without naming the cost. Cite Raymaker et al. (2020) and Hull et al. (2021) to clients who are inclined to "just power through."
  2. Do not conflate autism or ADHD with intellectual disability. They are orthogonal. Coach to the individual.
  3. Do not assume the client wants to disclose. Disclosure is the client's decision.
  4. Do not recommend "just power through." Cognitive and sensory load are real, not weakness.
  5. Do not recommend specific medications, ever. Even general comments cross into clinical advice.
  6. Do not promise the candidate disclosure will help. The evidence is favourable on average — but stigma is still documented. Disclosure is a calculated bet, not a guarantee.
  7. Do not let "fit" go unchallenged when an employer rejects a qualified neurodivergent candidate on those grounds. "Fit" is the most common vector for bias in unstructured interviewing.

Accommodations & How to Request Them

~12 min read · Procedural rights, scripts, and timing

Not legal advice The framework summarized below — duty to accommodate, AODA notification requirements, the BFOR test — is general training, not legal advice. For real legal questions, refer clients to a licensed Ontario lawyer or to the Human Rights Legal Support Centre (free; 1-866-625-5179).

The procedural floor (Ontario)

Ontario's Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (O. Reg. 191/11) requires employers to:

  • Tell the public and employees that accommodation is available in recruitment;
  • Tell applicants individually, when inviting them to assessments, that accommodation is available on request;
  • Consult with the applicant to provide suitable accommodation that takes their disability into account.

A client's request for an interview accommodation is procedurally protected. It is not a favour. Many clients don't know this — name it.

Common interview accommodations to request

Drawn from AskEARN, Autistica, CADDAC, LDAO, JAN, OHRC policy, and provincial human-rights commission materials:

  1. Questions in advance — 15 minutes to 48 hours, depending on need
  2. Written agenda — interviewer names, roles, format, expected duration
  3. Single interviewer rather than panel — or explicit panel introductions and name cards
  4. Quiet room with controllable lighting; option to turn off overhead fluorescents
  5. Permission to bring notes, a fidget object, water
  6. Breaks as needed
  7. Work-sample task as primary or supplementary assessment
  8. Written or asynchronous follow-up if recall is the issue
  9. Explicit behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time when…") rather than abstract hypotheticals
  10. Plain language in job descriptions, distinguishing "required" from "preferred"
  11. ASL/LSQ interpreter or CART captioning
  12. Extended time on written assessments (commonly 1.25× or 1.5× standard)
  13. Alternate format (large print, electronic with screen-reader compatibility, Braille)
  14. Accessible venue — confirm before arrival
  15. Specific time of day — for clients with cognitive fatigue, chronic illness, or medication timing

The accommodation-request email — structure

  1. Thank the employer for the invitation.
  2. Briefly name the accommodation needed. You do not need to name the disability — naming the need is enough.
  3. Specify exactly what you're requesting.
  4. Offer to discuss alternatives.
  5. Confirm logistics (date, time, format).

Send to the HR contact or recruiter on the invitation, not the hiring manager. Send as soon as the interview is confirmed — not the morning of.

When the employer pushes back

Most accommodation requests are granted without friction. When they aren't, coach the client through the levels:

  1. Restate the request and offer flexibility. "I understand the standard format. Would it be possible to use [accommodation] as an alternative — or another reasonable substitute that works for your process?"
  2. Name the procedural right calmly. "Ontario's IASR requires that employers consult with applicants on suitable accommodation. Could we discuss what would work on your side?"
  3. If the request is refused outright, the client can (a) accept it and proceed under the standard format, (b) decline the interview, or (c) document and contact HRLSC. This is a client decision, not a counsellor decision.

Cost — outside funding can resolve the "undue hardship" objection

Where an employer cites cost concerns, several Ontario and federal programs partially or fully fund workplace accommodations and assistive technology, including the Government of Canada's Opportunities Fund for Persons with Disabilities and provincial assistive-devices programs. Counsellors should know these exist and refer rather than negotiate on the client's behalf.

What counsellors don't do here

  • We don't disclose on the client's behalf without documented consent.
  • We don't draft accommodation requests and send them from our own email — the client owns the conversation.
  • We don't negotiate accommodation terms once an offer is on the table; that's an HR conversation, with legal advice if needed.

Virtual & AI-Screened Interviews

~12 min read · Live remote, async, and AI screening

Live remote (Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet)

The default first-round format for most office-track and many service-sector roles since 2020.

Technical setup

  • Camera at eye level. Stack books under a laptop if needed. Looking down at the camera reads as low confidence.
  • Light in front, not behind. Window facing you is best; window behind you puts you in silhouette. If only a side light is available, position it slightly off-centre in front so it lights both halves of your face.
  • Look at the camera lens when speaking; glance at the participant tile only when listening. Some candidates drag the interviewer's video tile directly under their webcam to reduce eye-drift.
  • Test the link 10–15 minutes early. Have the recruiter's phone number as backup.
  • Frame head-and-shoulders — top of head near top of frame, shoulders visible.
  • Mute notifications on the device. Close email, Slack, browser tabs that ping.
  • Wired earbuds or a headset reduce echo and improve audio.
  • Wired internet if possible. If on Wi-Fi, position close to the router.

Coaching priorities specific to video

  • Slow down. Audio compression and slight lag punish fast speech.
  • Pause longer than feels natural. The interviewer may have a half-second of lag; jumping in too quickly causes overlap.
  • Use the chat sparingly. Mostly stick to voice. The chat is fine for technical clarification ("link to my portfolio: …").
  • Print the resume and job description. Glancing down at paper reads better on camera than scrolling another window.

Asynchronous (one-way) video

The candidate records answers to pre-set prompts with no live interviewer. Used heavily for high-volume early screening. Major platforms include HireVue, Modern Hire (now part of HireVue), and Spark Hire.

Format

  • Question appears on screen; 30–60 seconds of think-time; 1–3 minutes to record per answer; 1–2 retakes usually allowed.
  • You may not see your interviewer's face at all. Speak as though they were friendly and engaged.

Prep

  • Re-read the job description right before — the keywords you mirror will be matched against it.
  • STAR but tight: roughly 15s situation + task, 60s action, 15s result.
  • Look into the camera lens, not at your own image.
  • Plain wall background; light from the front; no notifications.
  • Do the platform's tech check if offered.

Pitfall

Over-rehearsing into rigidity. Async penalizes flat delivery as much as live does. Memorized monotone is worse than slightly imperfect spoken-as-you-think.

AI-screened video — what actually happens (post-2021)

In January 2021, HireVue publicly stopped using facial-expression analysis in its assessments after internal review and FTC pressure from the Electronic Privacy Information Center (Maurer, 2021; Mickle, 2021). Internal HireVue research found nonverbal/visual cues contributed roughly 0.25% of predictive power in most models, rising to about 4% in customer-facing roles.

What current AI screening actually evaluates:

  • Speech-to-text transcripts of answers — keyword/skill matching against the job competency model.
  • Linguistic features of responses — vocabulary, response structure, completeness.
  • Game-based assessments (cognitive/personality), separate from the video.

What HireVue states it does NOT (post-2021) evaluate:

  • Facial expressions to infer emotion or "trustworthiness."
  • Skin tone, gender, age.

Coaching implication: what candidates say, and how clearly they structure it, is what gets scored. Tell clients to speak in complete sentences, use STAR (or STAR-L for failure questions), and mirror keywords from the job posting.

Ontario AI-disclosure under the 2026 rules Under the Working for Workers Four Act, 2024 (effective January 1, 2026), Ontario employers with 25+ employees must disclose in publicly advertised job postings whether AI is used to screen, assess, or select applicants. Coach clients to read postings carefully and ask the recruiter directly if it's unclear.

Accessibility in virtual interviews

  • Live captioning in Zoom/Teams/Meet is widely available. Clients can enable it on their side without requesting accommodation; for an interpreter or CART captionist, that's a request to make in advance.
  • Asynchronous platforms vary in accessibility. Screen-reader compatibility, captioning of pre-recorded prompts, and the ability to upload an alternate-format response are not universal. If a client needs a specific accommodation for an async platform, request it before recording.
  • Camera-off requests are a legitimate accommodation for some clients (eye-contact load, masking fatigue, body-image anxiety related to a disability, religious considerations). Frame as: "I'd like to do this interview audio-only — could you confirm that works on your side?"

Presence & Body Language

~10 min read · With the pop-psych myths debunked

This module is short on purpose. A lot of what's been taught about interview body language is wrong, and the corrective is mostly subtraction.

What to drop from older curriculum

Three claims to stop teaching
  1. "93% of communication is nonverbal" / the Mehrabian 55-38-7 rule. The original 1967 Mehrabian studies involved single spoken words and tested only situations of inconsistent communication of feelings and attitudes. Mehrabian himself has repeatedly stated the figures do not generalize to ordinary communication (Lapakko, 1997; Trager, 2008).
  2. "Power posing" as a hormonal intervention. The original Carney, Cuddy & Yap (2010) finding has failed multiple high-powered replications (Ranehill et al., 2015). First author Dana Carney publicly stated in 2016: "I do not believe that 'power pose' effects are real" (Carney, 2016). Recommend removing from curriculum.
  3. "Interviewers decide in the first 7 seconds." Catchy but not what research shows. Barrick, Swider, & Stewart (2010) found first impressions in structured interviews correlate with final ratings but the data does not establish any specific second-count.

What credible sources actually recommend

  • Eye contact. Make warm, regular eye contact when speaking and listening. In video, look into the lens. "Stare-down" reads as aggressive; "no eye contact" reads as low confidence or dishonesty. No validated percentage rule exists — coach as a range, not a metric. For autistic clients, see Module 9.
  • Posture. Sit up, feet flat, lean slightly forward when listening. "Open" posture (uncrossed arms) reads as engaged.
  • Handshake (in-person). Post-COVID norm: let the interviewer initiate. If they extend a hand, return a firm-but-not-crushing handshake with eye contact. If they nod, wave, or step back, mirror that. Both are socially acceptable.
  • Smiling. Genuine smiles at greeting and when discussing positive topics increase rated likeability. Continuous smiling reads as nervous.
  • Hands. Visible, occasional natural gesture. Hidden hands (under the table) read as anxious or hiding something.
  • Voice. Audible, paced, with downward intonation at the ends of statements (rising intonation reads as a question even when it isn't).

The congruence principle (what to keep from the Mehrabian work)

The genuinely useful part of Mehrabian's research is the corollary: when verbal and nonverbal cues contradict, listeners trust the nonverbal. So a candidate saying "I love teamwork" while looking down and frowning reads as a lie. The coaching frame:

Congruence — the only Mehrabian principle worth teaching "Aim for congruence. Your face, voice, and posture should match what you're saying. Interviewers notice mismatches — and they trust the nonverbal."

Dress

One half-step above the role's daily dress code. For office roles, business casual is now default; the recruiter will tell you when business formal is expected (government, law, banking, executive). For trades, retail, warehouse, and food service, "clean and tidy" beats "suit." When in doubt, ask the recruiter — coach the client to ask.

Cultural humility — say it again

The norms above are culturally specific. Coach honestly — these are what most North American interviewers will expect — but acknowledge that for some clients (newcomer, Indigenous, neurodivergent), these norms feel like instructions to perform a foreign culture. Give the client room to decide how much of themselves they're willing to translate. The job of an interview is to land the offer, but a client who lands an offer by erasing themselves often pays the cost later in retention.

Closing Strong & Follow-up

~10 min read · The last 10 minutes are part of the scorecard

"Do you have questions for us?" — not optional

This closing question is scored. A candidate who says "No, I think you've covered it" lowers their final rating — it reads as low interest or low preparation. Coach 4–5 prepared questions. Expect 2–3 will actually get asked because the interviewer will have answered the rest during the conversation.

Strong questions for the candidate to ask

From HBR's curated list (Glickman & Cipriano, 2022) and other reputable career sources:

  1. "What does success in this role look like at 30, 60, and 90 days?"
  2. "What are the biggest challenges the person in this role will face in the first six months?"
  3. "How would you describe the team's working style?"
  4. "What's the path for growth or development from this position?"
  5. "How is performance measured here, and how often is feedback given?"
  6. "Why is this position open?" (Reveals whether it's growth, turnover, or a problem hire.)
  7. "What do you enjoy most about working here?" (Personalizes the interviewer.)
  8. "What are the next steps in the process and when can I expect to hear back?"

What to avoid in early rounds

  • Salary, benefits, vacation, work-from-home flexibility — unless the employer raised them first, or the role is hourly and these are standard early questions.
  • Questions Googleable in 5 seconds ("What does your company do?").
  • Anything that signals you didn't read the posting.

The closing line — what the candidate says

Coach a clear, confident closing:

Closing line "Thank you for the conversation. After hearing more, I'm very interested in this role — particularly the [specific thing discussed]. I'd love to be considered for the next step. Is there anything else I can provide to help you decide?"

Thank-you notes

Industry surveys of HR managers report that a majority say thank-you notes factor into their decision, while only a minority of candidates actually send one (Robert Half industry survey, as widely cited; treat as industry consensus rather than peer-reviewed research). This is one of the highest-leverage low-effort moves in a job search.

Best practice

  • Format: email is standard. Handwritten note is a nice bonus only if you can deliver same-day in industries where it fits (small local employers, some service businesses).
  • Timing: within 24 hours of the interview, before the decision window closes.
  • Length: 4–6 sentences.
  • Content: thank them, reference one specific thing discussed, briefly restate fit, signal continued interest.
  • One per interviewer if a panel — vary the specific reference. Don't copy-paste.

See Appendix D for a ready-to-send template.

Following up appropriately

  • If the employer gave a timeline, wait one business day past it before following up.
  • If no timeline, wait 5–7 business days after the interview (after sending the thank-you).
  • One follow-up email, polite, restating interest. Then wait another week. After two unanswered follow-ups, move on. Continued silence is a soft "no."

See Appendix D for a ready-to-send follow-up template.

The 45-day rule (Ontario, 2026)

Under the Working for Workers Four Act, 2024, Ontario employers with 25+ employees who use publicly advertised job postings must notify interviewed applicants within 45 days of the last interview whether a hiring decision has been made. If a client passes that window with no word, a polite follow-up referencing the rule is reasonable — and often unblocks a stalled decision.

Module 14

Salary Conversations

~10 min read · 2026 Ontario pay-transparency context

What changed January 1, 2026

Under Ontario's Working for Workers Four Act, 2024, employers with 25+ employees must — for every publicly advertised posting — disclose expected compensation or a compensation range. The range cannot span more than $50,000. The disclosure requirement does not apply to roles where the expected compensation, or the top of the range, exceeds $200,000 per year (Government of Ontario, 2024; Littler, 2025; Stikeman Elliott, 2025).

What this means for clients: the salary range is often on the posting itself. Coach clients to read it before the interview and walk in with an anchor.

Common myth — Ontario does NOT have a salary-history ban Unlike British Columbia and Prince Edward Island, Ontario's 2026 framework does not include a ban on asking candidates about prior salary. An Ontario employer can still ask, "What were you earning at your last job?" Many clients have heard otherwise from U.S. media. Coach the answer, don't assume the question is illegal.

Researching market rate before the interview

  • The posting itself — now the most authoritative source under the 2026 rules.
  • Job Bank Canada wage data (jobbank.gc.ca) — federal wage information by occupation and region.
  • Glassdoor, Indeed Salary, LinkedIn Salary Insights, PayScale — crowdsourced, useful as triangulation. Treat any single source skeptically.
  • Union scales for unionized trades.
  • Public-sector salary disclosure (Sunshine List) for any Ontario public-sector role >$100,000.

Answering "What are your salary expectations?"

The three-step approach

  1. Research the market first.
  2. Give a range, not a single number. Recommended spread of $5,000–$10,000.
  3. Defer if asked too early. "I'd love to learn more about the role and scope before I commit to a number — can we revisit this once we've talked through the responsibilities?"

Sample responses

Early in the process (deferring) "I'd rather understand the scope first. Based on the range you've posted and what I'm seeing for similar roles in [region], I'm comfortable in that range — happy to talk specific numbers once we're closer to an offer."
Late in the process (committing) "Based on my research and the value I'd bring — particularly [specific strength relevant to the role] — I'm looking in the range of $X to $Y. I'm open to discussing how that fits with your structure."

Answering "What were you earning at your last job?"

Legal in Ontario, but the candidate doesn't have to take the question literally:

Deflecting salary-history "I'd rather focus on the value I'd bring to this role. Based on what I've researched for similar positions in [region], I'm looking in the range of $X to $Y."

Negotiating after the offer

Almost every offer has some room. Most candidates don't ask. Coach the client through a single, polite counter:

  1. Thank them. "I'm excited about the role."
  2. Anchor your ask. "Based on [market research / years of experience / specific skill], I was hoping we could discuss the salary closer to $[10–15% above the offer]."
  3. Be ready to discuss non-cash items. Vacation, signing bonus, professional development, work-from-home days, equipment allowance, start date. Many employers can move on these when they can't move on salary.
  4. Don't shop offers you don't have. "I'm waiting on another offer" works only if you actually are.

What counsellors don't do

We don't negotiate salary on behalf of the client. We don't tell the client what they're worth. We don't make the call between accepting and walking. We prepare the client to do all of that themselves.

Module 15

Practice Scenarios & Case Studies

~15 min read · Best worked through in pairs or as a team

Scenario A — "Jordan," 28, autistic, applying for QA analyst

Jordan has a college diploma in software testing and four months of contract work via a temp agency. He has had three first-round interviews in the last year, no second rounds. He is autistic, formally diagnosed at 23. He doesn't make sustained eye contact, often answers questions very literally, and has been told twice that he "didn't seem enthusiastic." He wants to disclose his autism but is afraid it will hurt him.

Discuss: What does the evidence say about pre-interview disclosure for autistic candidates? What format change might help (panel vs single)? Which question types are likely to trip him up, and how do we rehearse converting abstract questions to behavioural ones? What employer-specific programs in the region might match his profile?

One way to approach this
  • Disclosure: Sasson & Morrison (2019) and Whelpley & May (2023) both found disclosing autism improves interviewer ratings. With Jordan's consent, draft a brief pre-interview email naming the diagnosis, the accommodations he'd find helpful (questions in advance, single interviewer, no fluorescent lighting), and one strength-frame line. Send to the recruiter, not the hiring manager.
  • Format change: request a single interviewer rather than panel. Panel interviews multiply the eye-contact load and the social-fluency penalty.
  • Question prep: rehearse converting abstract questions to behavioural ones. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" → answer it as "Tell me about a time you set a learning goal and worked toward it."
  • Programs: Specialisterne Canada and Ready, Willing and Able both have QA / tech-track partnerships with major Canadian employers. Worth a referral.
  • Don't: coach him to "mask" — give performances of eye contact and enthusiasm. The masking literature is clear on the burnout cost.

Scenario B — "Marie," 47, returning to work after caregiving, severe interview anxiety

Marie left a part-time cashier role in 2018 to provide full-time care for her father, who passed away last year. She has not worked since. She has had two interviews in the last six months and froze in both — went silent, couldn't recall prepared answers, left feeling humiliated. She is applying for a medical receptionist role and is starting to avoid scheduling interviews altogether.

Discuss: Is this within the scope of interview coaching alone, or is a referral warranted? What evidence-based techniques would you teach first? What in-the-moment script could she use when she feels herself going blank? How do you structure mock interviews to actually habituate the anxiety rather than just sample it?

One way to approach this
  • Refer in parallel: the avoidance pattern is the clinical red flag. BounceBack (CMHA Ontario) or Ontario Structured Psychotherapy referral while continuing employment work.
  • First-line techniques: reappraisal as excitement ("I am excited" out loud, pre-interview); slow-paced breathing (4 in, 6 out); implementation intentions ("if my mind goes blank, then I will sip water and breathe out slowly").
  • In-the-moment script: "Can I take a moment to think?" — paired with the water-sip pause and an implementation intention so it's automatic, not a decision under stress.
  • Mock interview structure: at least three sessions, escalating realism. Session 1: seated with counsellor, conversational. Session 2: across-the-desk, video on, formal timing. Session 3: with a stranger she hasn't met, real timing, real outfit, video review afterward. Expect anxiety to rise in early sessions — that's the work.
  • Frame for Marie: "Your job is not to feel ready. It's to show up and do the next thing. The feelings will catch up."

Scenario C — "Ahmed," 34, newcomer, civil engineering background, panel interview ahead

Ahmed was a project engineer for 8 years in his home country before immigrating to Canada 14 months ago. His credential is being assessed by Professional Engineers Ontario. He has been invited to a panel interview for a site coordinator role at a Hamilton-area construction firm. He is nervous about the panel format and worried about being asked illegal questions about his background.

Discuss: How does he handle his foreign credential without overstating Canadian licensure? What does the panel-interview prep look like (name cards, sweep eye contact)? How does he handle the indirect "Where are you from?" question if it comes up? What Ontario-specific terms might come up that he should know?

One way to approach this
  • Credential framing: avoid "Engineer" / "P.Eng." in his answers — those are protected titles in Ontario. Use "Civil engineering background (assessed by PEO)" and frame the site-coordinator role as a deliberate Canadian-experience step.
  • Panel prep: ask the recruiter for interviewer names and roles ahead. Bring a small index card. Address the question-asker primarily; sweep the panel briefly mid-answer; close looking at the asker.
  • "Where are you from?": this is the OHRC-flagged indirect question. Redirect: "I moved to Canada from [country] 14 months ago — I'd love to talk about how my international project experience translates to the work here." Move the conversation forward without escalating.
  • Ontario-specific terms to know: CCDC 2 stipulated-price contracts, Ontario Building Code references, Form 1000 (OHSA constructor registration), MTO contract specs.
  • Cover-letter / interview opening: "I bring 8 years of project engineering experience from [country]. I'm building my Canadian site-coordinator base while completing my PEO licensure process."

Scenario D — "Priya," 41, ADHD, mid-career IT shift

Priya has 10 years as a help-desk lead. She just finished an AWS Cloud Practitioner certification and wants to move into a cloud-support associate role. She has ADHD (diagnosed in her 30s) and her main interview struggle is going off on tangents — she has prepared STAR examples but loses the thread mid-answer.

Discuss: What coaching strategies work for tangential answers? How does she leverage her ADHD strengths in framing? What accommodations might she request — and would she want to disclose? How do you structure a question bank that plays to working-memory limits?

One way to approach this
  • Tangent-recovery script: rehearse the implementation intention "If I notice myself rambling, then I will pause and say 'Let me bring that back to your question.'" This works when it's automatic, not when it's a decision under stress.
  • STAR cards: one per story, single key sentence at the top, key Action bullets. Permission to glance during the interview.
  • Strength framing: "I'm best in environments with novelty and parallel work — help desk taught me to triage and switch context. Cloud support is a natural extension of that."
  • Accommodation: permission to bring notes, water, and a fidget. Whether to disclose ADHD by name is her call — the accommodation can be requested without naming it.
  • Pre-interview movement: a brisk walk before arriving discharges motor energy.

Scenario E — "Devon," 28, deaf candidate, async video interview

Devon is profoundly deaf and uses ASL as his primary language. He has been invited to complete a one-way video interview through a HireVue-style platform for a warehouse logistics role. The platform has no captioning of the recorded prompts and no option to submit a video in ASL with subtitles.

Discuss: What's the accommodation request here? Who does he send it to? What alternatives are reasonable? What if the employer pushes back?

One way to approach this
  • The request: email the recruiter (not the platform vendor) as soon as the invitation arrives. Name the accessibility gap, request an alternative: either (a) written prompts + written/video response, (b) live remote interview with ASL interpreter, or (c) in-person interview with interpreter. Offer to discuss what works on their side.
  • Procedural right: under Ontario's IASR, the employer must consult on suitable accommodation. The async platform's design constraints are not the candidate's problem to solve.
  • If pushed back: escalate to the HR contact in writing; document the exchange; if refused, Devon can decline the interview and document for potential HRTO application. HRLSC (free) can advise.
  • Coaching note: Devon's frustration with platform inaccessibility is legitimate; the employer chose the platform, not Devon. Validate before strategizing.

Scenario F — "Karen," 52, layoff after 22 years, never been to a behavioural interview

Karen worked the same line at the same plant since age 30. The plant closed; she's never had a "behavioural" interview — her last interview was a 20-minute chat with the plant foreman in 2002. She has been invited to a structured interview for a quality-control role at a competing manufacturer.

Discuss: How do you compress 24 years of "haven't done this" into useful prep? What STAR examples can she surface from 22 years of one role? How do you address the emotional weight of the layoff and the unfamiliarity of the format simultaneously?

Group exercise — Rewrite this answer

Start with: "My greatest weakness is that I'm a perfectionist. I just care too much about getting things right."

In small groups, rewrite this as a real, modest, work-relevant weakness with a concrete corrective action. Each group produces one rewrite for: (1) a warehouse picker, (2) a medical receptionist, (3) a junior software developer, (4) a team lead. Compare answers. What's common across the good rewrites?

Appendix B

Glossary

Reference · Plain-language definitions of terms used in this course

AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)
Tools and methods that supplement or replace speech — picture boards, speech-generating devices, sign language, typing. Used by some clients with speech-affecting disabilities.
Accommodation
A change to a process, environment, or task that allows a person with a disability to participate equally. Required under the Ontario Human Rights Code to the point of undue hardship.
ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)
An evidence-based therapeutic approach that emphasizes accepting difficult emotions while still acting on values, rather than trying to control or eliminate the emotions.
ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)
A neurodevelopmental condition characterized by patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and/or impulsivity that affect functioning.
AODA
The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005 — Ontario legislation aiming at accessibility through enforceable standards.
ASL / LSQ
American Sign Language / Langue des signes québécoise — the primary sign languages of Deaf communities in English and French Canada respectively.
Asynchronous (one-way) video interview
An interview format in which the candidate records answers to pre-set prompts with no live interviewer. Common platforms include HireVue, Modern Hire, Spark Hire.
Attentional Control Theory
Eysenck et al.'s account of how anxiety preferentially consumes working memory and inhibitory control resources, increasing the cognitive cost of performance.
Behavioural interview
An interview format that asks "Tell me about a time when…" — focused on past behaviour as predictor of future behaviour. Scored against a competency rubric.
BFOR (Bona fide occupational requirement)
A standard or rule an employer can demonstrate is genuinely necessary for performance of the job; the only defence to discriminatory impact under the Meiorin test.
CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation)
Live captioning of speech, typically by a trained captionist. Common accommodation for Deaf, hard-of-hearing, and some neurodivergent candidates.
CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)
The most-studied evidence-based psychotherapy for anxiety and depression. Identifies and tests catastrophic automatic thoughts; pairs cognitive restructuring with behavioural exposure.
Competency-based interview
Synonym for structured behavioural interview — questions tied to specific job competencies, scored against a rubric.
Constructive discrimination
Discrimination that arises from a neutral rule or standard that has an exclusionary effect on a protected group. Addressed by section 11 of the Ontario Human Rights Code.
DCD (Developmental Coordination Disorder) / Dyspraxia
A neurodevelopmental condition affecting motor coordination; under-recognized in adults.
Disclosure
A candidate's voluntary communication of a disability to an employer. Can be full, partial, or withheld; can occur pre-interview, during, or post-offer.
DSM-5-TR
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition, Text Revision (American Psychiatric Association, 2022) — the standard reference for psychiatric diagnoses in North America.
Duty to accommodate
The employer's legal obligation to adjust standards, rules, or environments to allow people with disabilities to participate equally, to the point of undue hardship.
Essential duties
The vital, indispensable parts of a job — distinct from preferred or marginal tasks. The reference point for accommodation analysis under the Code.
HRTO (Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario)
The independent adjudicative body that hears applications alleging discrimination under the Ontario Human Rights Code. One-year limitation period; filing is free.
HRLSC (Human Rights Legal Support Centre)
Independent Ontario-funded agency providing free human-rights legal advice to anyone in Ontario regardless of income.
IASR (Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation)
O. Reg. 191/11 under AODA. Sets the procedural floor for accessibility in employment, recruitment, customer service, transportation, design of public spaces, and information & communications.
IAP (Individual Accommodation Plan)
The written, documented plan required under section 28 of IASR for employees with disabilities at employers with 50+ employees.
Implementation intention
Gollwitzer's "if X, then Y" planning structure that converts intentions into reliable behaviour. Strong evidence base across cognitive, affective, and behavioural outcomes.
MASI (Measure of Anxiety in Selection Interviews)
McCarthy and Goffin's 30-item scale measuring five dimensions of interview anxiety: communication, appearance, social, performance, and behavioural.
Masking / Camouflaging
The practice of suppressing autistic (or other neurodivergent) behaviour to appear neurotypical. Associated with anxiety, depression, exhaustion, and "autistic burnout."
Meiorin test
The unified three-step BFOR analysis articulated by the Supreme Court of Canada in British Columbia (PSERC) v. BCGSEU, 1999.
OHRC (Ontario Human Rights Commission)
The provincial body that issues policy guidance under the Ontario Human Rights Code (distinct from the Tribunal, which adjudicates individual cases).
Panel interview
One candidate, multiple interviewers — common in government, healthcare, education, unionized environments, and senior roles.
PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder)
A trauma- and stressor-related disorder per DSM-5-TR. Can be triggered by interview-room dynamics for clients with relevant histories.
SAD (Social Anxiety Disorder)
A diagnosable anxiety disorder characterized by marked, persistent (≥6 months) fear of social situations involving possible scrutiny. Distinct from everyday interview nerves.
STAR / STAR-L
Situation, Task, Action, Result (+ Learning) — the dominant taxonomy for behavioural interview answers, originating with Development Dimensions International's Targeted Selection methodology.
Structured interview
An interview in which every candidate receives the same questions in the same order, scored against a rubric. Has substantially higher predictive validity than unstructured interviews.
Tourette syndrome (TS)
A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by multiple motor tics and at least one vocal tic for over a year. Most people with TS do not have coprolalia (involuntary swearing).
Undue hardship
The threshold beyond which an employer is not required to accommodate. Under section 17(2) of the Code, the only recognized factors are cost, outside sources of funding, and health & safety.
Working interview / trial shift
A real shift the candidate works as part of the hiring process. Generally compensable in Ontario under the Employment Standards Act.
Working for Workers Four Act, 2024
Ontario Bill 149; the legislation that introduced the 2026 job-posting transparency requirements (pay range, AI disclosure, existing-vacancy disclosure, ban on Canadian-experience requirements).

Appendix D

Scripts & Templates

Reference · Ready-to-adapt scripts for clients

These are starting points, not finished communications. Strip every placeholder and adapt to the client's voice before sending. Templates are released under CC-BY: copy, adapt, and reshape them freely for client work.

1. Accommodation request — ASL interpreter

To: recruiter on the interview invitation

Subject: Interview on [date] — accommodation request

Hello [recruiter],

Thank you for inviting me to interview for [position] on [date]. I'm Deaf and use ASL. To participate fully, I'd like to request an ASL interpreter for the interview. I can recommend interpreter agencies if helpful, or you can arrange through your usual provider.

Please confirm by [date] so we can finalize logistics. Happy to discuss if there are any questions.

Thank you,
[Name]

2. Accommodation request — extended time on a written assessment

To: recruiter or HR contact

Subject: [Position] assessment — accommodation request

Hello [name],

Thank you for the invitation to complete the written assessment for [position]. I have a [disability category — e.g., "learning disability that affects processing speed"] and would like to request 1.5× standard time, which is the accommodation I have used previously.

Please let me know if you'd like to discuss further.

Thank you,
[Name]

3. Accommodation request — questions in advance

To: recruiter

Subject: Interview prep — accommodation request

Hello [recruiter],

Thank you for the invitation to interview. I'm [autistic / have ADHD / etc.], and one accommodation that helps me show my best work is receiving the interview questions in writing [15 minutes before / 24 hours before] the interview.

Please let me know what you're able to arrange — happy to discuss alternatives if helpful.

Thank you,
[Name]

4. Accommodation request — sensory / quiet room

To: recruiter

Subject: Interview on [date] — accommodation request

Hello [recruiter],

Thank you for the invitation to interview. Two small accommodations that help me show my best work: (1) holding the interview in a quiet room without overhead fluorescent lighting where possible, and (2) being introduced to each panel member by name and role at the start so I can take a brief note.

Please confirm what you're able to arrange.

Thank you,
[Name]

5. Accommodation request — alternate format (async video → live or written)

To: recruiter

Subject: [Position] — interview format accommodation

Hello [recruiter],

Thank you for inviting me to complete the one-way video interview. The platform doesn't support [captioning / screen-reader compatibility / ASL response / etc.], which is the accommodation I need to participate fully. Could we arrange one of the following alternatives: (a) a live remote interview with [interpreter / captioning], (b) written responses to the same prompts, or (c) an in-person interview?

Happy to discuss what works on your side.

Thank you,
[Name]

6. Disclosure script — noticeable disability (in-person interview opening)

Spoken, after greeting and introductions

"You'll notice I [use a wheelchair / use a white cane / have a service animal / have a facial scar from a burn — name what's noticeable]. For the duties we've discussed, my current setup works without any changes from you; for [specific duty if applicable], I use [specific accommodation]. Happy to answer any logistics questions, and then I'd love to get into the questions for the role."

7. Disclosure script — not-noticeable disability (when accommodation is needed)

In writing before the interview, or spoken at the start

"I want to mention that I have [condition — e.g., 'a chronic-pain condition,' 'an anxiety disorder,' 'ADHD']. It doesn't affect my ability to do this work, but one small accommodation helps me show my best: [name the specific accommodation]. With that in place, [specific strength relevant to the role] is part of why I've succeeded in [prior context]."

8. Disclosure script — stutter, tics, or speech difference

Spoken, at the very start of the interview

"Before we start, I want to mention that I [stutter / have tics from Tourette syndrome / have a speech difference]. You may notice [some repetitions / some involuntary movements or sounds / slower speech in places]. It doesn't affect my skills. Feel free to ask me to repeat anything, and please don't try to finish my sentences — I've got it. Ready when you are."

9. Thank-you email (send within 24 hours)

To: each interviewer (vary the specific reference)

Subject: Thank you — [Role] interview

Dear [Name],

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [Role] position. I especially appreciated learning about [one specific thing the interviewer mentioned — a project, a team challenge, a tool they use]. Our conversation reinforced my interest in the role, and I believe my experience with [relevant skill] would let me contribute quickly.

Please let me know if any further information would be helpful as you move forward.

Sincerely,
[Name]

10. Follow-up email (after the timeline window)

If the employer gave a timeline, send one business day after. If no timeline, send 5–7 business days after the thank-you.

Subject: Following up on [Role] interview

Dear [Name],

I'm following up on my interview for the [Role] position on [date]. I remain very interested in the role and would welcome any update on the timeline or next steps.

Please let me know if there's anything further I can provide to support your decision.

Thank you,
[Name]

Coaching note These templates are starting points. The most important coaching move is to have the client say the words aloud — written-only scripts often don't translate to the room. For accommodation requests, have the client read it aloud before sending. For spoken disclosure scripts, rehearse with you three times before the interview.
